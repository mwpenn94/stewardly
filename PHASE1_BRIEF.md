## 0.5 What Stewardly is — the foundational framing

Stewardly is **infrastructure that supports all AI, AI-agent, and related tooling**. It is not a vertical SaaS product that happens to be in the financial-advisor space. The wealth specialization is currently the most operationally developed instance of the infrastructure being put to use; it is not the destination.

Three principles flow from this framing and govern every Phase 2 decision below.

**First, the five engines (Formational, Relational, Missional, Contextual, Continuous-Improvement) are ontologically complete.** They are first-principles engines that cover the universe of user cases through which stewardship work flows: how people learn and grow (Formational); how people connect to and serve each other (Relational); how people apply their work to a purpose, often nested into mission specializations such as Wealth, Coaching, Pastoral, Teaching, Healthcare, and Community (Missional); how the right thing surfaces at the right moment (Contextual); and how the whole system gets better over time (Continuous-Improvement). The design accepts that today's implementation is operationally imperfect and is continuously improved through the Continuous-Improvement engine itself acting on the platform. Ontological completeness means there is no user case that falls outside the five-engine surface; operational imperfection means today's coverage is partial and improving.

**Second, the platform delivers great out-of-box benefit AND honors bring-your-own (BYO) AI, agents, and tooling as equal first-class paths.** A user who arrives without their own AI provider, integration credentials, or extended tooling gets immediate value from the bundled defaults: a bundled internal-AI orchestration (note: this is *not* Manus-as-default per C-27 below; Manus is one provider among many on the BYO side), the shipped engine applets with sensible defaults, and the pre-loaded reference data. A user who arrives with their own AI provider, their own model, their own agents, or their own integrations plugs them in as first-class citizens of the same infrastructure. Both paths are honored by the **three-component pricing model** detailed in C-2 above: the customer's invoice equals `ValueShare(MeasuredSavings) + Cost-Plus on app/operational expenses + provider DirectCost (path-dependent)`, capped at the cost-plus-equivalent customer-protection ceiling. The path-dependence is precise: on the internal-bundled path Stewardly contracts with the provider on the customer's behalf and includes provider DirectCost in the cost-plus base; on the BYO path the customer pays the provider directly through their own contract and the provider DirectCost component is therefore zero. Both paths preserve Stewardly's profitability floor through the always-present Cost-Plus on app/operational expenses component, so the platform never runs unprofitably regardless of which path a customer chooses or which stage of the M\&V calculation is operating. Neither path subsidises or penalises the other; on the BYO path Stewardly's economic interest is in fact aligned *with* the user's chosen provider's success, because more capable providers drive higher MeasuredSavings, which drives higher ValueShare (see C-24 below).

**Third, the engine applets ship turned-on within hierarchy controls and constraints.** A new tenant arrives with the five engines available, populated with sensible defaults that are usable on day one. From there, every user at every layer of the administrative hierarchy can **create new instances, read and inspect, update behavior, delete or archive, and personalize** engine applets to themselves and to those below them in the hierarchy, within the controls and constraints set by those above them. The hierarchy is **five structural layers — Tenant > Org/Firm > Team/Supervisor > Professional > Client — plus an orthogonal delegate-scope dimension** that any layer's principal can use to grant a strictly-subset permission envelope to a delegate (a Professional delegating to an Assistant, a Tenant administrator delegating to a deputy, a Firm administrator delegating to a deputy, a Team supervisor delegating to an acting supervisor, a Client delegating to an authorized representative). Each of the five layers is anchored to a *structural unit*; the assistant pattern is a delegate-scope of Professional, not a sixth layer. Delegate-scope is a property of a relationship, not a layer in the hierarchy, and the same machinery serves all five layers without proliferating layer-specific rules. The CRUD-and-personalize affordance applies at each of the five layers AND through delegate-scopes (a Professional can CRUD their own engine-applet personalizations, and can also CRUD the delegated subset they grant their Assistant). The CRUD-and-personalize affordance is itself an architectural commitment, not a feature backlog item, because it is what makes the infrastructure usable as infrastructure rather than as an opinionated SaaS product.

These three principles modify the brief in three ways: they elevate the cost-plus pricing model and the M&V two-stage methodology from "Wave C" to foundational; they add five new commitments (C-21 ontological completeness with continuous-improvement, C-22 infrastructure-with-out-of-box-and-BYOM-equal, C-23 CRUD-and-personalize-at-every-layer, C-24 dual-anchor strategic non-competition with foundation-model providers, C-25 Hub and TaskChat as complementary first-class front doors over a shared persistent substrate) to the existing twenty; and they reframe the wealth specialization as an exemplar of the pattern rather than as the product itself.

## 0.7 Three-dimensional dynamism — what "infrastructure" actually means in code

The infrastructure framing in §0.5 is not abstract. It resolves into three orthogonal axes of dynamism that the architecture has to support concurrently. Naming them explicitly here governs how Phase 2 spends effort.

**Horizontal dynamism — the universe of bring-your-own AI, agents, and related tooling.** The platform supports continuous improvement *horizontally* in accommodating the entire universe of AI providers, agents, and related tooling that users want to plug in. New providers can be added without architectural surgery; existing providers can be deepened, replaced, or removed without breaking dependent engines. User accessibility and delightful ease of use are first-order requirements of this axis: a user must be able to bring a new model and reach productive use within minutes, not days, and without writing code. The Continuous-Improvement engine acts on this axis by surfacing where new providers should be added, where existing provider integrations have decayed, and where the shipped defaults can be tuned for better out-of-box experience.

**Vertical dynamism — the three-component pricing model and the two-stage M\&V methodology, applied path-aware across both BYO and internal-bundled consumption.** The platform supports continuous improvement *vertically* across two parallel paths simultaneously, both governed by the same three-component pricing formula (`ValueShare + Cost-Plus on app/operational expenses + provider DirectCost path-dependent`, capped at the cost-plus-equivalent ceiling, per C-2). On the **internal-bundled path**, the customer pays all three components: ValueShare on validated efficiency gains, Cost-Plus on Stewardly's app and operational expenses, and Cost-Plus on provider DirectCost which Stewardly bears on the customer's behalf. On the **BYO path**, the customer pays only the first two components: ValueShare on validated efficiency gains, and Cost-Plus on Stewardly's app and operational expenses; the provider DirectCost component is zero because the customer pays the provider directly. Both paths use the same two-stage M\&V methodology (Stage 1 forward projection over Days 1–90 against industry baselines; Stage 2 empirical M\&V transitioning to 100% empirical against the customer's own baseline by Day 90) to compute MeasuredSavings, and both paths preserve Stewardly's profitability floor through the always-present Cost-Plus on app/operational expenses component. Internal capabilities exist to support the universe of capabilities *efficiently* — they never position to compete with foundation-model providers, and the pricing structure makes such competition economically self-defeating (see C-24 below).

**Extensibility dynamism — persistent surfaces, artifacts, files, and tools as first-class architectural citizens.** The platform extensibly accounts for persistent surfaces (the Hub), persistent artifacts (workspace tabs, generated documents, calculator outputs, exported plans), persistent files (uploaded documents, S3-stored media, voice recordings), and persistent tools (user-created applet instances, BYOM provider configurations, custom workflows). The Hub is positioned as the persistent-surface front door — a place where the user's accumulated artifacts, tools, and engine instances live and persist across sessions. The Home / TaskChat is positioned as the conversational front door — a place where the user converses, and which itself produces new artifacts and surfaces that flow back into the Hub. Both front doors are first-class peers anchored to the same persistent substrate (see C-25 below). Continuous-Improvement acts on this axis by improving artifact persistence, surface complementarity, and the round-trip from conversational creation to persistent surfacing.

These three axes are orthogonal: a Phase 2 item that improves any one of them should be evaluated for its impact on the other two. They are also enumerated in order of strategic load-bearing weight — horizontal dynamism is the existential commitment (without it the infrastructure positioning collapses), vertical dynamism is the economic commitment (without it the cost-plus differentiator is rhetorical), and extensibility dynamism is the user-experience commitment (without it the platform feels disposable rather than accumulative).

## 2. Architectural commitments (C-1 through C-27)

The original twenty commitments from the prior brief are preserved unchanged in numbering and intent. Seven new commitments are added (C-21 through C-27) to capture the user's framing clarifications across two Phase 1 cycles: ontological completeness with continuous improvement (C-21); infrastructure-with-out-of-box-and-BYO-equal (C-22); CRUD-and-personalize-at-every-layer (C-23); dual-anchor strategic non-competition with foundation-model providers (C-24); Hub and TaskChat as complementary first-class front doors over a shared persistent substrate (C-25); technological completeness — the architecture commits to filling the industry gap of dynamically supporting the universe of current and future AI capabilities and surfaces (C-26); and provider-neutral BYO including Manus as one provider among many (C-27). Evidence columns are expanded with cross-source attribution: where a capability already exists in the deployed v3, in `stewardly-ai`, in `emba_modules`, or in `manus-next-app`, that source is named so Phase 2 can re-use rather than re-build.

**Pricing model — definitive three-component reconciliation.** The user's framing across messages, validated against the AFK mandate (Pasted_content_01.txt §064–L079) and the existing code (`server/engines/_substrateAdapters.ts`), resolves the pricing model into a three-component formula that applies path-aware on the internal-bundled and BYO consumption paths. The customer's invoice equals **ValueShare(MeasuredSavings) + Cost-Plus on app and operational expenses**, capped at the cost-plus-equivalent customer-protection ceiling. ValueShare is the primary revenue instrument; it captures Stewardly's share of validated efficiency gains computed by the two-stage M\&V methodology (Stage 1 forward projection Days 1–90 against industry baselines; Stage 2 empirical M\&V transitioning to 100% empirical by Day 90 against the customer's own baseline). Cost-Plus on app and operational expenses is the profitability floor; it recovers Stewardly's actual cost of running the platform (compute, storage, observability, internal substrate, support, infrastructure) with a defined markup so the platform never runs unprofitably regardless of MeasuredSavings outcomes in any billing period. The third component, **provider DirectCost**, is path-dependent: on the **internal-bundled path**, Stewardly contracts with the provider on the customer's behalf and includes provider DirectCost in the cost-plus base; on the **BYO path**, the customer pays the provider directly through their own contract, Stewardly never touches that money flow, and the provider DirectCost component is therefore zero (no markup is applied to a cost Stewardly does not bear). The customer-protection ceiling enforces that the customer is never invoiced above the cost-plus equivalent for the same workload regardless of path or stage. This three-component formulation reconciles the AFK mandate's "unified pricing formula" (which describes the conceptual structure) with the user's bifurcation (which describes how the components populate across paths) with the user's profitability-floor refinement (which clarifies that Cost-Plus must always cover app and operational expenses); it also resolves the apparent tension in AFK L079 "identical Stewardly economics across all four BYOM scenarios" — "identical economics" means identical contribution margin per unit of customer value delivered, not identical price-formula components. C-2 below is restated in these terms; C-22 below states the BYO path's profitability preservation; C-24 below states why this pricing structure makes foundation-model competition economically self-defeating; judgment call **D-M** in §5 records the reconciliation.

### 2.3 Summary tally

The platform has the right structural skeleton and the right deployed surface area. What it lacks falls cleanly into four buckets:

**Foundational gaps that block the infrastructure-positioning claim.** C-6 (pricing), C-7 (M\&V two-stage), C-9 (protection ceiling), C-22 (BYO as equal path), C-24 (dual-anchor non-competition: provider-DirectCost zero-markup codified, the rest of the economic anchor and the compliance anchor unbuilt), C-26 (plug-and-play affordance layer for the universe of surfaces and providers), C-27 (provider-neutral abstraction layer so Manus is one provider among many rather than the *de facto* default by inheritance) — without these, the three-component pricing differentiator does not exist in code, the strategic non-competition posture is unenforceable, and the technological-completeness and provider-neutrality claims are aspirational rather than architectural. **Architectural gaps that block trust in the engines as engines.** C-3 (lint), C-10 (executable conflict-of-interest), C-11 (principle test), C-18 runtime (Quantic blocklist), C-21 (Formational and Contextual applet UI; CI-self-application), C-25 (shared-substrate anchoring; Hub-TaskChat parity invariant). **Re-incorporation opportunities from the legacy sources.** Formational depth from `emba_modules`; relational depth and integrations from `stewardly-ai`; portable patterns from `stewardly-command-center`. These cluster in Wave E.

## 5. Architectural decisions I intend to make (judgment calls, with rationale)

### D-N. Hierarchy structure (Reconciliation C, locked by user)

**Decision:** the layered identity model is **five structural layers — Tenant > Org/Firm > Team/Supervisor > Professional > Client — plus an orthogonal delegate-scope dimension** that any layer's principal can use to grant a strictly-subset permission envelope to a delegate. The assistant pattern is implemented as a `DelegateScope` of Professional, not as a sixth layer.

**Rationale:** each of the five structural layers is anchored to a *structural unit* (tenant, org, team, professional, client). None is defined relative to its relationship to another role, so the hierarchy stays orthogonal to delegation. Delegation generalizes the assistant pattern so the same machinery serves Professional → Assistant, Tenant admin → deputy tenant admin, Firm admin → deputy firm admin, Team supervisor → acting supervisor, and Client → authorized representative without proliferating layer-specific rules. The five-`/settings/*` route shape evidenced in the deployed v3 (`/tenant/settings`, `/org/settings`, `/team/settings`, `/professional/settings`, `/client/portal-or-onboarding`) maps cleanly to the five layers. The `mwpenn94/stewardly-ai` `planningHierarchy.ts` shape corroborates the five-layer reading. The `/admin/feature-permissions` page becomes the place where principals configure delegate-scopes.

**Trade-off that would cause me to surface this back to you:** if a future scenario requires a permission scope that is genuinely structural rather than delegated (e.g., a regulatory regime mandates that compliance officers be a separate layer rather than a delegated role of a Firm admin), I would re-open this judgment call rather than try to force the new role into delegate-scope semantics.

**Reverses earlier draft:** this supersedes the prior six-layer description (Tenant > Firm-admin > Supervisor > Advisor > Assistant > Client) that appeared in earlier brief drafts. Any remaining six-layer references in the codebase are technical debt to be cleaned up in Wave A.

### D-M. Three-component pricing reconciliation

The pricing model is reconciled as follows after multiple framing iterations from the user. **The customer's invoice equals `ValueShare(MeasuredSavings) + Cost-Plus on app and operational expenses + provider DirectCost (path-dependent)`, capped at the cost-plus-equivalent customer-protection ceiling.** This three-component formulation reconciles three sources that initially appeared in tension: the AFK mandate's "unified pricing formula" with "customer never invoiced above cost-plus equivalent" (Pasted_content_01.txt L066–L075), which describes the conceptual structure; the user's **bifurcation** message which clarified that internal-app and BYO paths price differently in practice (internal-app = cost-plus + efficiency-gains; BYO = efficiency-gains-only); and the user's **profitability-floor** message which clarified that Cost-Plus must always cover Stewardly's app and operational expenses so the platform never runs unprofitably regardless of MeasuredSavings outcomes. The reconciliation: ValueShare is the primary revenue instrument and applies on both paths; Cost-Plus on app and operational expenses is the profitability floor and applies on both paths; provider DirectCost is the only component that is path-dependent — on internal-bundled it is non-zero and Stewardly applies the cost-plus markup to it (because Stewardly contracts with the provider), on BYO it is zero (because the customer contracts with the provider directly and Stewardly never touches that money flow). The customer-protection ceiling ("never above cost-plus equivalent for the same workload") is preserved on both paths and across both M\&V stages. "Identical Stewardly economics across all four BYOM scenarios" (AFK L079) is interpreted as identical contribution margin per unit of customer value delivered, not identical price-formula components. The economic non-competition anchor in C-24 follows from this reconciliation rather than from cost-plus alone: foundation-model competition would require capturing token-volume scaling margin, but Stewardly's three components scale with infrastructure cost (sub-linear), customer outcome (decoupled from token volume), and provider DirectCost (path-zeroed on BYO) — none of which deliver foundation-model economics. **Phase 2 implementation order:** the three-component formula is built in Wave A as the unified pricing module before any other pricing logic; the path-aware behavior is built in the SubstrateRouter; the customer-protection ceiling is built as an invariant test that runs before invoice emission; the AFK L079 "identical Stewardly economics" claim is validated in Wave A by a parametric model that compares contribution margins across all four BYOM scenarios using shared assumptions. If user direction supersedes any portion of this reconciliation in Phase 2, the formula is updated and the parametric validation is re-run.
