## Slide 2 — The Thesis
**Headline:** 2026 is the year AI moved from suggestion to action.
**Body:** For a decade, enterprise AI meant an assistant that suggested. In 2026, AI became a colleague that executes. The market, the investment, and the architecture have all re-based around autonomous agents that plan, act, and reflect — and the transition happened faster than any prior enterprise-software shift, including cloud.

**Supporting pillars (three short lines):**
- A $10.9B market growing at ~46% CAGR through 2030.
- 51% of enterprises already run agents in production.
- 40% of enterprise apps will embed task-specific agents by year end.

## Slide 7 — Anatomy of a modern agent
**Headline:** The four-part architecture that became a de-facto standard.
**Body:** A production-grade AI agent in 2026 is no longer a single model call. It is a loop: a **planner** decomposes the goal, a **memory** layer (short-term context and long-term vector stores) supplies relevant facts, a **tool router** dispatches to web search, code sandboxes, SQL, and enterprise APIs, and a **critic** reflects on results before the next step. Around this core sit the real differentiators for enterprise deployment: guardrails (AuthN/Z, PII/DLP, audit logs) and observability (traces, evals, cost and latency).

## Slide 8 — ROI and productivity
**Headline:** The ROI case is no longer speculative.
**Body:** Two-thirds of EMEA enterprises surveyed by IBM in late 2025 reported significant productivity gains from AI, and a quarter said the impact was transformative. Conversational and voice agents are on pace to save roughly $80B in global contact-center costs by 2026. Specific case studies are no longer abstractions: Linde reported a 92% reduction in audit-report preparation time after deploying agents in the finance workflow (MIT HDSR, 2026). That pattern — 80%+ cycle-time reductions in narrow, well-scoped workflows — is now the template for ROI justification.

## Slide 9 — The unglamorous 80%
**Headline:** Capability is solved. Governance is not.
**Body:** For every headline about capability, there is a quieter headline about governance. Writer.com's 2026 survey reports that 79% of organizations face challenges in adopting AI, a double-digit jump from 2025. Industry security reports indicate that only a small fraction of deployed agents have formal security approval. Gartner warns that more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by the end of 2027 — not for lack of capability, but for unclear value, weak risk controls, and runaway costs. The winners of the next two years will be the organizations that treat observability, evals, and access control as first-class features of their agent platforms.

**Supporting stats:**
- 79% — organizations facing AI adoption challenges in 2026 (Writer.com).
- 14% — share of deployed agents with formal security approval (industry reports, Mar 2026).
- >40% — agentic AI projects Gartner expects to be canceled by end of 2027.

## Slide 10 — The mandate for 2026
**Headline:** From experiments to infrastructure — with discipline.
**Body:** The era of the AI agent has arrived. The question for enterprise leaders is no longer whether to deploy agents, but how to deploy them safely, observably, and at scale. Three moves define the year ahead: (1) consolidate scattered pilots into a small number of well-governed agent platforms; (2) invest in evals and observability before scaling tool access; and (3) treat the human-in-the-loop design as a product decision, not a compliance checkbox. The organizations that get this right will see AI as infrastructure. The rest will see it as a line item that keeps getting canceled.
